\section{Introduction}
Hierarchical clustering is a way to recursively partition data into clusters. Given a weighted graph $G = (V, E, c)$, where $c\colon E\to \mathbb{N}$ denotes the \emph{cost} of cutting an edge, the goal is to find a rooted tree called \textit{clustering tree} $T$, in which every node $u\in V\br{T}$ represents a connected component $G_u\subseteq G$ called a \textit{cluster}. Moreover, every non-leaf node $u$ also corresponds to a subset of edges $S_u$ such that each child node $v$ of $u$ is then associated with a cluster of $G_u\setminus S_u$. For each leaf node $l\in V\br{T}$ it is required that $G_u$ is a singleton. The \textit{Dasgupta's clustering objective} is defined as $\COST_G\br{T} = \sum_{u\in V\br{T}} c(S_u) \cdot |V(G_u)|$\footnote{Note that the usual way of defining the Dasgupta's clustering objective is $\COST_G\br{T} = \sum_{uv\in E} c(uv) \cdot |\ell_T\br{u,v}|$, where $\ell_T\br{u,v}$ denotes the smallest subtree containing both $u$ and $v$, but these are equivalent.}. The problem of finding a hierarchical clustering tree with minimum cost is known as the \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering} problem. Among possible interpretations of this clustering cost is the average summaric cost of edges being cut on the path from root of $T$ to a leaf of $T$ representing a single cluster containing a vertex drawn uniformly at random.

One can see the clustering tree as a way of recursively partitioning data until all clusters become singletons. In this work we focus on a relaxed variant of the above problem, in which we modify the halting condition of this recursive process. Instead of stopping when all edges are deleted, the clustering tree stops when the remaining cluster is a graph of some class $\cF$, which is given as a part of the input. Therefore we only require that for each leaf node $l\in V\br{T}$, $G_u\in \cF$. We call this problem \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering into $\cF$-clusters} (HC-$\cF$). \DD{A może by nazwać problem krocej jako \textsc{Hierarchical $\cF$-Clustering}? \subsection{Motivations and Applications}
Hierarchical clustering is a clusterization technique far richer in its structure and applications then the traditional flat clustering. Since such an object encodes a hierarchical decomposition of data, one may treat each nodes of $T$ as a way to encode iteratively refined ways of partitioning the input domain. For example any phylogenetic tree is a hierarchical clusterization as well as file systems, management hierarchies of companies, socio-political divisions of people and many more. Relaxing the halting condition of the clustering process has some intuitive applications both in case of trees as well as in case of bounded diameter graphs. The former case is due to the fact that trees are among simplest classes of graphs and therefore most of their structural properties are well understood. Moreover, many problems which are hard on general graphs can be solved efficiently on trees. Since the usual clustering tree consists of cutting all edges of the input graph, such an object might become too large to store. Since many real life networks are sparse, it is sensible to ask whether we can get a more compact representation of the hierarchical decomposition of the data. Bounded diameter instances are interesting sincce such clusters consists of aggregation of points which are close to each other and it is often desirable to have such clusters in the final clustering tree.

Among possible usages of the clustering into trees, we can devise a heuristic for the \textsc{HC} problem by solving the \textsc{HC-$\cT$}. The idea is as follows: we firstly run the algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} to get a clustering tree $T$, such that each leaf cluster $A$ of $T$w is a tree. Then, for each such $A$ we run any algorithm for \textsc{HC} on $A$ to find the remaining levels of clusterization. Since \textsc{HC} of a tree with a constant factor error can be found in polynomial time \cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics} the most expensive part of the cost of the clustering tree will be the cost of the \textsc{HC-$\cT$}. Therefore, the better approximation algorithm we have for \textsc{HC-$\cT$}, the better the performance of the heuristic becomes. 
Note that one can also use this heuristic dynamically, in the sense that the \textsc{HC-$\cT$} clustering tree can be stored statically and whenever any cluster needs to be refined, we can run the \textsc{HC} algorithm on the corresponding tree cluster. This way we can get a partially dynamic clustering tree occupying sparser space then the full \textsc{HC} tree, while still being able to refine clusters on demand.

We also observe that despite similariies, the objective of \textsc{HC-$\cT$} can be much lower then the objective of \textsc{HC}. Let $G$ by a star with one of the vertices of the star being replaced by a triangle. Assume all of the costs are uniform. The optimal clustering tree for \textsc{HC} will have cost $\Omega\br{n^2}$, since at least $(n-2)/2$ vertices have need to have at least $(n-2)/2$ edges cut before they become singletions. On the other hand, the optimal clustering tree for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} will have cost $n$, since the optimal clustering consists of cutting one edge of the triangle. This shows that \textsc{HC-$\cT$} can be significantly different from \textsc{HC}.

\subsection{Our results and techniques}

In this work we present an $O\br{\log n \cdot \log\log n}$-approximation algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} and $O\brc{\log n}$-approximation algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cD$}. Conceptually, our work follows a general technique we called \textit{flattening} which consists of subdividing a problem of finding a hierarchical object such as a clustering tree into a problem of finding a sequence of flat objects such as separators or cuts. Among multiple uses of such a technique, we can mention \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}~\cite{ACostFunctionForSimilarityBasedHierarchicalClustering, ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics,Hierarchical_Clustering_Objective_Functions_and_Algorithms}, \textsc{Graph Search Problem}~\cite{Approximating_the_Average_Case_Graph_Search_Problem_with_Non_uniform_Costs}, \textsc{Tree Decompositions}~\cite{Approximating_Treewidth_Pathwidth_Frontsize_and_Shortest_Elimination_Tree}, \textsc{Precedence-Constrained Decision Tree}~\cite{Precedence-Constrained_Decision_Trees_and_Coverings} and many more.

Our ideas are particularly inspired by the work of Charikar and Chatziafratis~\cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics}, who used the flattening technique in conjunction with an SDP relaxation of an ILP formulation of \textsc{HC}, which they decompose to obtain a series of solutions for $k$-Balanced Partitioning SDP of \cite{PartitioningGraphsIntoBalancedComponents}. We refine their technique by devising a similar, but much more complex ILP. This is due to the fact that we need to force both cycle breaking as well as efficient graph partitioning. To achieve the first goal we add constraints similar to the ones used in the ILP formulation of \textsc{Feedback Edge Set}/\textsc{Min-multicut}. In order to extract useful information from our ILP, we use a two-fold way of relaxing it, one for each goal we wish to achieve. Firstly, we use an LP relaxation combined with a simple arithmetic rounding procedure in order to fix the subsets of vertices which need to belong to a $\cF$-cluster at a given level. Upon doing that, we use a further SDP relaxation in order to obtain information about the edges we need to cut in to decrease the size of the instance efficiently. These two measures of progress are crucial for the performance of our algorithm. The first one allows us to make sure that we are most important verticse end up in $\cF$-cluster quickly. The second one allows us to iteratively decrease the size of the instance, so that the cost of the cuts made by the clustering tree is not charged multiple times at a given level. During the recursive processing our algorithm uses the solutions to the relaxations in an alternating manner until all of the clusters become acyclic. Using a careful lower bounding of the cost we get that the approximation ratio of our algorithm is asymptotically the same as the approximation ratio achieved by the rounding procedure for \textsc{FES}/\textsc{MMC} LP which is $O\br{\log n \cdot \log\log n}$ \cite{ApproximatingMinimumFeedbackSetsAndMulticutsInDirectedGraphs} for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} and $O\br{\log n}$ for \textsc{HC-$\cD$}. 

To complement the above result, we show that for both $\cT$ and $\cD$ the problem cannot be approximated within any constant factor under the Small Set Expansion Hypothesis~\cite{GraphExpansionAndTheUniqueGamesConjecture}. We use a blackbox reduction from the inapproximability of \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}~\cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics} by adding triangles to all of the vertices of the original graph. This reduction is interesting since it shows that intuitively, when the input graph contains multiple small cycles, the two problems behave in a very similar way. However, when the number of cycle is small, the example shown in the previous section suggests that the two problems can behave very different.
\subsection{Related Work}

\paragraph{Flat clusterization}
Classical flat clustering objectives include \textsc{$k$-Center}, \textsc{$k$-Median}, and \textsc{$k$-Means}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Center}, a simple greedy farthest-first traversal yields a $2$-approximation, and this factor is optimal unless $P=NP$ \cite{GonzalezKCenter, HochbaumShmoysKCenter}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Median}, local-search based methods achieve constant-factor approximations (in particular $9+\varepsilon$) \cite{AryaLocalSearchKMedian}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Means}, constant-factor approximations are also known (for example via local search), with improved guarantees in Euclidean settings \cite{KanungoKMeansLocalSearch, AhmadianKMeansImprovedApprox}.

\paragraph{\textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}}
Work on \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering} can be split into similarity-based and dissimilarity-based objectives. In the similarity-based setting, Dasgupta introduced the now standard objective and initiated its approximation-theoretic study \cite{ACostFunctionForSimilarityBasedHierarchicalClustering}. This line was further developed by Charikar and Chatziafratis, who obtained an $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation via \textsc{Sparsest Cut} and spreading-metric SDP relaxations and showed that any constant-factor approximation is NP-hard under the Small Set Expansion hypothesis \cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics}, and by Cohen-Addad et al., who studied objective-function variants and algorithmic guarantees \cite{Hierarchical_Clustering_Objective_Functions_and_Algorithms}. Another direction considers depth-based objectives, where one measures the depth of leaves or separators in the decomposition tree. For this type of objective, one gets a $O\br{\sqrt{\log n}}$-approximation on trees \cite{DereniowskiKosowskiUznanskiZouICALP2017}. For general graphs, combining a well known reduction from \textsc{Edge Ranking} \cite{RankingsOfGraphs1998} to \textsc{Vertex Ranking} with the current state of the art approximation for the latter \cite{Approximating_Treewidth_Pathwidth_Frontsize_and_Shortest_Elimination_Tree}, based on algorithm for\textsc{Vertex Separation} of \cite{FeigeHajiaghayiLee2005} yields an $O\br{\log^{3/2} n}$ guarantee.

In the dissimilarity-based setting, a dual objective to Dasgupta's cost is the \emph{revenue} objective. A sequence of approximation improvements for this objective includes $1/3$-approximation guarantees (for random splitting and average-linkage) by Moseley and Wang \cite{MoseleyWangNIPS2017}, an SDP-based improvement to $0.3364$ by Charikar, Chatziafratis, and Niazadeh \cite{CharikarChatziafratisNiazadehSODA2019}, a $0.4246$ guarantee via \textsc{Max-Uncut Bisection} by Ahmadian et al. \cite{BisectAndConquer2020}, and then a $0.585$-approximation by Alon, Azar, and Vainstein \cite{AlonAzarVainsteinCOLT2020}.

\paragraph{\textsc{Feedback Edge Set}}
\textsc{Feedback Edge Set} admits both minimization and maximization variants. In the minimization form, one removes a minimum-weight set of edges to destroy all cycles (or all cycles through a specified terminal set, as in our formulation). In the dual maximization form, one keeps a maximum-weight acyclic subgraph. Approximation algorithms for weighted minimization variants are well studied. In particular, Even et al.\ obtained an $O(\log n \cdot \log\log n)$-approximation for weighted \textsc{Feedback Edge Set} and \textsc{Multicut} formulations \cite{ApproximatingMinimumFeedbackSetsAndMulticutsInDirectedGraphs}, which is the best known ratio for general instances and a key ingredient in our algorithm. The directed variant is APX-hard (NP-hard to approximate within a factor of $1.3606$, via L-reduction from \textsc{Minimum Vertex Cover}), and no constant-factor approximation is known. For the dual maximization form (\textsc{Maximum Acyclic Subgraph}), a simple ordering argument yields a $1/2$-approximation.

\paragraph{\textsc{Balanced Partitioning}}
This area is tightly connected to \textsc{Sparsest Cut} and \textsc{Balanced Cut}: Leighton and Rao obtained an $O(\log n)$-approximation for \textsc{Sparsest Cut} via multicommodity max-flow \cite{LeightonRao1999}, later improved to $O(\sqrt{\log n})$ by Arora, Rao, and Vazirani via SDP and geometric embeddings \cite{AroraRaoVazirani2009}. For \textsc{Minimum-Weight Balanced Vertex Separator}, Feige, Hajiaghayi, and Lee obtained an analogous $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation \cite{FeigeHajiaghayiLee2005}. Exact $(k,1)$-\textsc{Balanced Partitioning} (equal-size components) admits no polynomial-time approximation within any finite factor unless $P=NP$ \cite{AndreevRacke2004}, motivating the search of bicriteria approximation algorithms. Krauthgamer, Naor, and Schwartz gave an $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation of the cut cost while allowing component sizes up to $2n/k$, based on an SDP relaxation \cite{PartitioningGraphsIntoBalancedComponents}. For the Minimum-Multicut problem tightly connected to partitioning graph into balanced components, Garg, Vazirani, and Yannakakis obtained an $O(\log n)$-approximation via a linear programming relaxation \cite{ApproximateMaxFlowMinMultiCut1993}.
