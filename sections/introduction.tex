\section{Introduction}
Hierarchical clustering is a way to recursively partition a dataset into clusters. Given a weighted, connected graph $G = (V, E, c)$, where $c\colon E\to \mathbb{N}$ denotes the \emph{cost} of cutting an edge, the goal is to find a rooted tree called \textit{clustering tree} $T$, in which every node $u\in V\br{T}$ represents a connected component $G_u\subseteq G$ called a \textit{cluster}. Moreover, every non-leaf node of $T$, $u$ also corresponds to a subset of edges $S_u$ such that each child node $v$ of $u$ is then associated with a cluster which is a connected component in $G_u\setminus S_u$. For each leaf node $l\in V\br{T}$ it is required that $G_l$ is a singleton, i.~e., $\spr{V\br{G_l}}=1$. Then, the \textit{Dasgupta's clustering objective} is defined as $\COST_G\br{T} = \sum_{u\in V\br{T}} c(S_u) \cdot |V(G_u)|$\footnote{Note that the usual way of defining the Dasgupta's clustering objective is $\COST_G\br{T} = \sum_{uv\in E} c(uv) \cdot |\ell_T\br{u,v}|$, where $\ell_T\br{u,v}$ denotes the smallest subtree containing both $u$ and $v$, but our formulation is equivalent.}. The problem of finding a hierarchical clustering tree which minizes the above cost is known as the \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering} problem. Among possible interpretations of this objective function is the average summaric cost of edges being cut on the path from root of $T$ to a leaf of $T$ representing a single cluster containing a vertex drawn uniformly at random.

One can see the clustering tree as a way of recursively partitioning data until all of the cluster become graphs with some desireable property. Classically, we require each leaf cluster to become a singleton. In this work we modify the halting condition of this recursive process to stop when the remaining cluster is a graph of some class $\cF$ which is given as a part of the input. Therefore we only require that for each leaf node $l\in V\br{T}$, $G_l\in \cF$. We call this problem \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering into $\cF$-clusters} (HC-$\cF$). The goal is again to minimize $\COST_G\br{T} = \sum_{u\in V\br{T}} c(S_u) \cdot |V(G_u)|$. \subsection{Motivations and Applications}
Hierarchical clustering is a clusterization technique far richer in its structure and applications than the traditional flat clustering. Since such an object encodes a hierarchical decomposition of data, one may interpret $T$ as a way to encode iteratively refined ways of partitioning the input domain. Phylogenetic trees, file systems, management hierarchies of companies and socio-political divisions of people are all examples of hierarchical clusterization. Relaxing the halting condition of the clustering process has some intuitive applications both in case of trees as well as in case of bounded diameter graphs. The former is due to the fact that since the usual clustering tree consists of cutting all edges of the input graph, such an object might become too large to store. Moreover, many real life networks are sparse, it is sensible to ask whether we can get a more compact representation of the hierarchical decomposition of the data. 
% Trees are among simplest classes of graphs and therefore most of their structural properties are well understood. Additionally, many problems which are hard on general graphs can be solved efficiently on trees. 
Bounded diameter instances are interesting since such clusters consist of aggregation of points which are close to each other, which is often desirable, since this means that the clusters are more or less homogeneous. 

Among possible usages of clustering into trees is the following: We can devise a heuristic for the \textsc{HC} problem by solving the \textsc{HC-$\cT$}. Firstly, run the algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} to get a clustering tree $T$, such that for each leaf $l$ of $T$, $G_l$ is a tree. Then, for each such $G_l$ we run any algorithm for \textsc{HC} of trees on $G_l$ to find the remaining levels of clusterization. Since \textsc{HC} of a tree with a constant-factor error can be found in polynomial time \cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics} the most expensive part of the cost of the clustering tree will be the cost of finding the \textsc{HC-$\cT$}. Therefore, the better approximation algorithm we have for \textsc{HC-$\cT$}, the better the performance of the heuristic becomes. 
Note that one can also use this heuristic dynamically, in the sense that the \textsc{HC-$\cT$} clustering tree can be stored statically and whenever any lwaf cluster needs to be refined, we can run the \textsc{HC} algorithm on it. This way we can get a partially dynamic clustering tree occupying substantially less space then the full \textsc{HC} tree, while still being able to refine clusters on demand.

We also remark that despite similarities, the objective of \textsc{HC-$\cT$} can be much lower then the objective of \textsc{HC}. Let $G$ by a star with one of the vertices of the star being replaced by a triangle. Assume all of the costs are uniform. The optimal clustering tree for \textsc{HC} will have cost $\Omega\br{n^2}$, since at least $(n-2)/2$ vertices have need to have at least $(n-2)/2$ edges cut before they become singletions. On the other hand, the optimal clustering tree for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} will have cost $n$, since the optimal clustering consists of cutting one edge of the triangle. This shows that \textsc{HC-$\cT$} can be significantly different from \textsc{HC}. To see a similar example for $\cD_d$-clusters, consider $d=1$ and an input graph which is a clique. 

\subsection{Our results and techniques}

In this work we present an $O\br{\log n \cdot \log\log n}$-approximation algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} and $O\br{\log n}$-approximation algorithm for \textsc{HC-$\cD_d$}. Conceptually, our work follows a general technique we called \textit{flattening} which consists of subdividing a problem of finding a hierarchical object such as a clustering tree into a problem of finding a sequence of flat objects such as separators or cuts. Among multiple uses of such a technique, we mention \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}~\cite{ACostFunctionForSimilarityBasedHierarchicalClustering, ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics,Hierarchical_Clustering_Objective_Functions_and_Algorithms}, \textsc{Graph Search Problem}~\cite{Approximating_the_Average_Case_Graph_Search_Problem_with_Non_uniform_Costs}, \textsc{Tree Decompositions}~\cite{Approximating_Treewidth_Pathwidth_Frontsize_and_Shortest_Elimination_Tree}, \textsc{Precedence-Constrained Decision Tree}~\cite{Precedence-Constrained_Decision_Trees_and_Coverings} and many more. 

Our ideas are particularly inspired by the work of Charikar and Chatziafratis~\cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics}, who used the flattening technique in conjunction with an SDP relaxation of \textsc{HC}, decomposed into a series of solutions to $k$-Balanced Partitioning SDP of \cite{PartitioningGraphsIntoBalancedComponents} to give an $O\br{\sqrt{\log n}}$-approximation for \textsc{HC}. We refine their technique by devising a similar, but much more complex relaxation. This is due to the fact that we need to force both cluster structure as well as efficient graph partitioning. To achieve the first goal we use constraints similar to the ones used in the ILP formulation of \textsc{Feedback Edge Set}/\textsc{Min-multicut}. To achieve the second goal we use constraints similar to the ones used in the SDP relxation of \textsc{$k$-Balanced Parition}. In order to extract useful information from our ILP, we use a two-fold way of relaxing it, one for each goal we wish to achieve. Firstly, we use an LP relaxation combined with a simple arithmetic rounding procedure in order to fix the subsets of vertices which need to belong to a $\cF$-cluster at a given level. Upon doing that, we use a further SDP relaxation in order to obtain information about the edges we need to cut in to decrease the size of the instance efficiently. These two measures of progress are crucial for the performance of our algorithm. The first one allows us to make sure that most important vertices end up in $\cF$-cluster quickly. The second one allows us to iteratively decrease the size of the instance, so that the cost of the cuts made by the clustering tree is not charged multiple times. During the recursive processing our algorithm uses the solutions to the relaxations in an alternating manner until all of the leaf clusters are $\cF$-clusters. Using a careful lower bounding of the cost we get that the approximation ratio of our algorithm is asymptotically the same as the approximation ratio achieved by the rounding procedure for \textsc{FES}/\textsc{MMC} LP which is $O\br{\log n \cdot \log\log n}$ \cite{ApproximatingMinimumFeedbackSetsAndMulticutsInDirectedGraphs} for \textsc{HC-$\cT$} and $O\br{\log n}$ for \textsc{HC-$\cD_d$}. 

To complement the above result, we show that for both $\cT$ and $\cD_d$ the problem cannot be approximated within any constant factor under the Small Set Expansion Hypothesis~\cite{GraphExpansionAndTheUniqueGamesConjecture}. Both proofs use a blackbox reduction from \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}~\cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics}. For $\cT$ this is done by adding triangles to all of the vertices of the original graph, and for $\cD_d$ this is done by adding a dense graph of diameter $d$ to all of the vertices of the original graph. 
\subsection{Related Work}

\paragraph{Flat clusterization}
Classical flat clustering objectives include \textsc{$k$-Center}, \textsc{$k$-Median}, and \textsc{$k$-Means}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Center}, a simple greedy farthest-first traversal yields a $2$-approximation, and this factor is optimal unless $P=NP$ \cite{GonzalezKCenter, HochbaumShmoysKCenter}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Median}, many constant factor approximation algorithms are known \cite{AConstant-FactorApproximationAlgorithmForTheKMedianProblem, ApproximationAlgorithmsForMetricFacilityLocationAndKMedianProblemsUsingThePrimal-DualSchemaAndLagrangianRelaxation, ANewGreedyApproachForFacilityLocationProblems, AryaLocalSearchKMedian, Approximating_k-Median_via_Pseudo-Approximation, AnImprovedApproximationForKMedianAndPositiveCorrelationInBudgetedOptimization,BreachingThe2LMPApproximationBarrierForFacilityLocationWithApplicationsToKMedian, ImprovedBi-pointRoundingAlgorithmsAndAGoldenBarrierFork-Median} culminating in $\br{2+\epsilon}$ approximation of \cite{A2-ApproximationAlgorithmForMetricKMedian}. For metric \textsc{$k$-Means}, constant-factor approximations are also known (for example via local search), with improved guarantees in Euclidean settings \cite{KanungoKMeansLocalSearch, AhmadianKMeansImprovedApprox} with the currently best known guarantee of $3+2\sqrt{2}+\epsilon< 5.83$ \cite{AnImprovedGreedyApproximationForMetricKMeans}.

\paragraph{\textsc{Hierarchical Clustering}}
Work on \textsc{Hierarchical Clustering} can be split into similarity-based and dissimilarity-based objectives. In the similarity-based setting, Dasgupta introduced the now standard objective and initiated its approximation-theoretic study \cite{ACostFunctionForSimilarityBasedHierarchicalClustering}. This line was further developed by Charikar and Chatziafratis, who obtained an $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation via \textsc{Sparsest Cut} and spreading-metric SDP relaxations and showed that any constant-factor approximation is NP-hard under the Small Set Expansion hypothesis \cite{ApproximateHierarchicalClusteringViaSparsestCutAndSpreadingMetrics}, and by Cohen-Addad et al., who studied objective-function variants and algorithmic guarantees \cite{Hierarchical_Clustering_Objective_Functions_and_Algorithms}. Another direction considers depth-based objectives, where one measures the depth of leaves or separators in the decomposition tree. For this type of objective, one gets a $O\br{\sqrt{\log n}}$-approximation on trees \cite{DereniowskiKosowskiUznanskiZouICALP2017}. For general graphs, combining a well known reduction from \textsc{Edge Ranking} \cite{RankingsOfGraphs1998} to \textsc{Vertex Ranking} with the current state of the art approximation for the latter \cite{Approximating_Treewidth_Pathwidth_Frontsize_and_Shortest_Elimination_Tree}, based on algorithm for\textsc{Vertex Separation} of \cite{FeigeHajiaghayiLee2005} yields an $O\br{\log^{3/2} n}$ guarantee.

In the dissimilarity-based setting, a dual objective to Dasgupta's cost is the \emph{revenue} objective. A sequence of approximation improvements for this objective includes $1/3$-approximation guarantees (for random splitting and average-linkage) by Moseley and Wang \cite{MoseleyWangNIPS2017}, an SDP-based improvement to $0.3364$ by Charikar, Chatziafratis, and Niazadeh \cite{CharikarChatziafratisNiazadehSODA2019}, a $0.4246$ guarantee via \textsc{Max-Uncut Bisection} by Ahmadian et al. \cite{BisectAndConquer2020}, and then a $0.585$-approximation by Alon, Azar, and Vainstein \cite{AlonAzarVainsteinCOLT2020}.

\paragraph{\textsc{Feedback Edge/Vertex Set}}
\textsc{Feedback Edge Set} 
When $X=V$, the \textsc{FES} is equivalent to Maximum Spanning Tree which can be found by modyfying the Kruskal's algorithm for the Minimum Spanning Tree. Otherwise, the problem is NP-hard and admits a $2$-approximatio \cite{ApproximatingMinimumSubsetFeedbackSetsInUndirectedGraphsWithApplications}. For directed graphs, the problem is known as \textsc{Feedback Arc Set}, is NP-hard and admits an $O\br{\log n \cdot \log\log n}$-approximation via an LP relaxation and rounding procedure of Even et al. \cite{ApproximatingMinimumFeedbackSetsAndMulticutsInDirectedGraphs}. The Feedback Vertex Set problem is NP-hard even for $X=V$ for which it admits a $2$-approximation \cite{A2-ApproximationAlgorithmForTheUndirectedFeedbackVertexSetProblem,OptimizationOfPearlsMethod} and a general $8$-approximation \cite{An8ApproximationAlgorithmForTheSubsetFeedbackVertexSetProblem}. For directed graphs, the problem is equivalent to \textsc{FAS}.

\paragraph{\textsc{Balanced Partitioning}}
This area is tightly connected to \textsc{Sparsest Cut} and \textsc{Balanced Cut}: Leighton and Rao obtained an $O(\log n)$-approximation for \textsc{Sparsest Cut} via multicommodity max-flow \cite{LeightonRao1999}, later improved to $O(\sqrt{\log n})$ by Arora, Rao, and Vazirani via SDP and geometric embeddings \cite{AroraRaoVazirani2009}. For \textsc{Minimum-Weight Balanced Vertex Separator}, Feige, Hajiaghayi, and Lee obtained an analogous $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation \cite{FeigeHajiaghayiLee2005}. Exact $(k,1)$-\textsc{Balanced Partitioning} (equal-size components) admits no polynomial-time approximation within any finite factor unless $P=NP$ \cite{AndreevRacke2004}, motivating the search of bicriteria approximation algorithms. Krauthgamer, Naor, and Schwartz gave an $O(\sqrt{\log n})$-approximation of the cut cost while allowing component sizes up to $2n/k$, based on an SDP relaxation \cite{PartitioningGraphsIntoBalancedComponents}. For the Minimum-Multicut problem tightly connected to partitioning graph into balanced components, Garg, Vazirani, and Yannakakis obtained an $O(\log n)$-approximation via a linear programming relaxation \cite{ApproximateMaxFlowMinMultiCut1993}.
